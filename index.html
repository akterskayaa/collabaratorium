01 Why it exists

Four fractures.
One response.

Something is broken — not in one place, but at every scale. The same split keeps appearing, differently dressed, in every field we work in.

01

Mind extracted from body

We think, analyse, and produce — but we have lost the intelligence that lives in movement, sensation, and physical presence. Cognitive work is prized; embodied knowing is treated as secondary.

02

Individual extracted from community

We have optimised for individual performance, individual credit, individual output. The result is isolated brilliance that cannot act coherently when the problem requires a collective.

03

Society extracted from nature

Our systems treat the living world as a resource rather than a home. The rituals and stories that once held us in relationship with the earth have been replaced by extraction logic.

04

Technology extracted from humanity

Our most powerful tools — AI, XR, biofeedback — are being built outside of ethical, cultural, and embodied frameworks. Technology accelerates; meaning does not keep pace.

These are not four separate problems.
They are one fracture happening at different scales — and they require a practice, not just a theory.

Collaboratorium is our response. A structured space where the process of working together is itself the design problem — and where new ways of being collective can be practiced, tested, and carried forward.

02 What we believe

The foundations
of our work

Before anything else — the ideas that shape every decision we make.

Story
Stories are the oldest technology humans have for connecting with each other. They create shared reference, shared feeling, shared imagination across disciplines, languages, and backgrounds. Story is not a method we apply — it is the medium through which people actually meet. Everything we design is structured as a journey.
Embodiment
Collective intelligence is not purely cognitive. How people move together, respond to each other, and hold shared space shapes the outcome as much as the ideas they generate. We work with body, emotion, and relation — not only with thinking. This is why we open and close every gathering with ritual. This is why breath and presence are part of the method.
Ritual
Ritual is a technology — one of the most powerful humans have ever developed for creating coherence in a group. Every collective that has built something meaningful has done it through repeatable, symbolic acts that align people around shared meaning. We are not recovering something mystical. We are recovering something practical that was lost when the enlightenment decoupled intelligence from ceremony.
Aesthetic literacy
The problems we face are systemic — but each field has locked its wisdom inside its own language and aesthetic. Aesthetic literacy is the capacity to move between these languages: to take what economics knows and apply it to ecology, to take what art knows and apply it to technology. This cross-disciplinary fluency is one of the most needed capabilities of our time.
Gift economy
Creative work flourishes when ideas circulate freely rather than being hoarded or commodified. Collaboratorium operates on the logic of the gift: what you bring is offered to the collective; what emerges belongs to the field. We are building a space where people who share this orientation can find each other and practice it together.
03 What it is

A lab and a
community of practice

Collaboratorium is both a time-bound creative lab and an ongoing community of practitioners. It lives inside Radiant IRIS — a cultural movement exploring how collective experience can be consciously designed.

Each seasonal cycle is a 3-week structured process bringing together a multidisciplinary group to design and prototype immersive collective experiences. Between cycles, the community continues — sharing language, methods, and relationships that outlast any single lab.

Collaboratorium members do not begin as roles.
They begin as stories.

Through a structured onboarding, each participant develops a personal myth — an archetypal character that becomes their entry point. From that moment, people meet through narrative and perspective, not through professional title. Skills return later in the process, as tools in service of the collective work.

This is not symbolic. It structurally changes what a group can access together — the quality of listening, the depth of collaboration, the willingness to take creative risks.

06 What it produces

Outputs inside.
Outputs beyond.

Each Collaboratorium cycle produces concrete things — and something harder to name: a different quality of collaborative intelligence in the people who went through it.

Embodied co-creation experience

Direct experience of non-hierarchical collaboration in real time. Working across disciplines through story, body, and ritual — not just tasks and deliverables.

Method

A transferable collective creation framework. The process developed through each cycle becomes available to carry into other contexts — inside your practice, your team, your community.

Prototypes and live experiences

Immersive environments, interaction systems, ritual formats, and narrative structures that can be developed into standalone projects, installations, or future Collaboratorium cycles.

Community capital

A trusted group of collaborators who share language, method, and values. Relationships that persist beyond the lab and form the basis for future work together.

07 Work from the field

Every project begins
with the same question

How do we design an experience that transforms people — not just moves them through a space? What will shift in them by the end?

Active project  ·  Collaboratorium coming soon
A Relational Layer for Existing Exhibitions
LACMA — Los Angeles County Museum of Art

Most museum visits are solitary — people move through the same space without meeting. We are designing a layer of interaction where visitors encounter each other and the art together: shared rituals, conversation structures, and collective formats. The connections they form become new artistic objects in themselves.

Core question: how do you turn parallel presence into genuine connection — and turn that connection into art?

Active project  ·  Collaboratorium coming soon
A Living Environment for Collective Experience
Fuji Sanctuary

Fuji Sanctuary becomes a living environment for collective experience. We design a journey of interconnected scenes where participants must align perception, make decisions together, and respond to emerging challenges. Moving through the landscape, the group shifts from individuals into a coordinated system — or fragments under pressure. The environment is not a backdrop. It is an active force shaping how the group perceives and acts.

Core question: what allows a group to sense and act as one within a shared environment?

Both projects are developed through the Collaboratorium process — multidisciplinary teams working across all three tracks toward prototypes that can be tested in the field.

08 Who we are looking for

Not a portfolio.
A resonance.

We are not selecting for the most impressive credentials. We are selecting for alignment — with the questions, the values, and the way of working.

The people who thrive in Collaboratorium tend to carry certain qualities. They are not roles to apply for — they are ways of moving through the world.

Cultural walkers

People who have moved across cultures, countries, or contexts — by choice or by circumstance. They know how to hold more than one perspective at once. They don't flatten difference; they navigate it.

Cultural wakers

People who are deeply themselves, wherever they are. Their authenticity makes things visible — patterns, assumptions, the places where the room is not telling the truth. They don't do it on purpose. They just can't pretend.

Cultural wayfinders

People who have learned to read rooms — not as performance, but from necessity. They sense power dynamics, emotional weather, what is unsaid. Often forged through difficult experience, this capacity becomes a rare gift.

Cultural weavers

People who draw connections between things that don't usually meet. They mix threads from different traditions, disciplines, and communities — and something new becomes visible in the weaving that was not there in any single strand.
